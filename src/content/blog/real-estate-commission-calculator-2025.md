Next, enter the listing agent commission. Most fall between 2.5% and 3%, but enter whatever rate you've negotiated. Then set the buyer agent commission separately. This matters in 2025. ## Average Real Estate Commission Rates in 2025: Expect 4.5%–5.4% Total

The 2024 NAR settlement pushed average commissions down. Before it, most sellers paid 5%–6% total. Now that number sits closer to 4.5%–5.4%, according to NAR's 2024 Member Profile.

Listing agent fees typically run 2.5%–3%. Buyer agent fees, now negotiated on their own, tend to fall between 2% and 3% depending on your market. High-value properties often carry lower percentages — a 2% fee on a $1.2 million home still produces $24,000, so agents accept less.

Discount brokerages have taken real market share. Redfin charges 1%–1.5% to list your home (as of 2025). Flat-fee MLS services put your property on the MLS — the database agents use to share listings — for a one-time payment of $300–$500, with no percentage attached (Redfin.com, 2025).

| Commission Type | Typical Range (2025) |
|---|---|
| Listing Agent | 2.5%–3% |
| Buyer Agent | 2%–3% |
| Discount Brokerage (Listing) | 1%–1.5% |
| Flat-Fee MLS | $300–$500 (one-time) |
| Total (Traditional) | 4.5%–5.4% |

Rural markets tend to run higher. Lower sale prices mean agents need a bigger percentage to cover time and marketing. Urban markets with expensive homes often see lower percentages but larger dollar totals. A 2023 Clever Real Estate survey found that agents in markets where median prices sit below $200,000 were the least likely to offer any discount.

## How the NAR Settlement Changed Commissions: Key Rules Since August 2024

In August 2024, the National Association of Realtors put new rules into effect as part of a $418 million settlement resolving antitrust lawsuits from home sellers (NAR official announcement, August 2024). The Department of Justice had been investigating commission practices since at least 2019. The settlement changed the basic structure of how agents get paid.

**The biggest change:** sellers no longer have to offer buyer agent compensation through the MLS. Before, listing a home on the MLS meant you had to name a buyer agent commission. That requirement is gone.

Buyers must now sign a written buyer representation agreement before touring homes. It spells out exactly what the buyer's agent earns and who pays it. Many sellers still offer buyer agent concessions to attract showings — but the amount is negotiable now, not assumed.

**Real-world example:** A seller in Chicago's Lincoln Park listed a $425,000 condo in early 2025. Under the old rules, she would have offered 2.5% to the buyer's agent through the MLS. Instead, she offered 2% and negotiated her listing agent down to 2.25%. Her total commission was 4.25% — saving her roughly $7,437 compared to the old 5% standard, and about $8,500 compared to a 6% arrangement.

One thing to watch: offering a below-market buyer agent concession can reduce traffic. A Redfin analysis from late 2024 found that listings offering less than 2% to buyer agents got 12% fewer showing requests in the first two weeks compared to listings offering 2.5% or more.

## How Agent Commission Splits Work Between Agent and Brokerage

When you pay 2.5% to your listing agent, that money doesn't all go to one person. The agent splits it with their brokerage — the company that holds their license and provides support, leads, and office resources.

Common split structures:

| Split Model | Agent Keeps | Broker Keeps | Common With |
|---|---|---|---|
| 70/30 | 70% | 30% | Mid-career agents |
| 80/20 | 80% | 20% | Experienced agents |
| 90/10 | 90% | 10% | Top producers |
| 100% (cap model) | 100% after cap | Monthly desk fee | eXp Realty, Keller Williams |

New agents often start at 50/50 or 60/40 and negotiate upward as they close more deals. Franchise brokerages like RE/MAX or Coldwell Banker may also pull a franchise fee — typically 3%–8% of the agent's gross commission — off the top before the split kicks in.

This affects you as a seller. An agent on a 50/50 split has more financial reason to hold firm on their rate. An agent on a 90/10 split or 100% cap model has more room to move. Asking about split structure during agent interviews can feel forward, but it quickly shows you who actually has flexibility on rate.

**One caveat:** a willingness to discount doesn't mean better service. A 2023 Zillow consumer survey found that 62% of sellers who negotiated a lower commission were satisfied with their agent — but satisfaction dropped when sellers picked an agent mainly based on lowest rate rather than track record.

## 6 Ways to Reduce Real Estate Commission Costs

### 1. Negotiate Directly With Your Agent

Commission rates have never been fixed by law. In 2025, more agents are willing to talk. If your home is priced above $500,000 or your market moves fast — under 30 days on market — you have real leverage. Many listing agents will accept 2%–2.25% rather than lose the listing entirely.

### 2. Use a Discount Brokerage

Several companies offer reduced listing fees (pricing as of 2025):

|---|---|---|
| Redfin | 1.5% (1% if buying too) | Full-service, salaried agents |
| Clever Real Estate | 1.5% flat | Matches you with local agents |
| Homie | $3,500 flat fee | Available in select states |
| Ideal Agent | 2% | Pre-vetted top agents |

(Source: Clever Real Estate and individual company websites, 2025)

### 3. Sell FSBO (For Sale By Owner)

You skip the listing agent commission entirely — potentially saving 2.5%–3%. The trade-off is real: you handle pricing, marketing, negotiations, and paperwork yourself. Only about 7% of home sales in 2024 were FSBO, and those homes sold for a median of $310,000 compared to $405,000 for agent-assisted sales (NAR 2024 Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers).

Part of that gap reflects the types of homes sold FSBO — many are lower-priced or sold between people who already know each other. So the comparison isn't clean. Still, FSBO sellers should budget for professional photography ($200–$500), a real estate attorney ($500–$1,500), and their own time running showings and handling offers.

### 4. Use a Flat-Fee MLS Listing

Flat-fee MLS services get your home on the MLS for $300–$500 with no percentage-based listing fee. You still control whether to offer buyer agent compensation, and you handle your own showings.

### 5. Consider an iBuyer (With Caution)

iBuyers like Opendoor let you sell in days with minimal showings. But their service fees typically run 5%–6%, and offers tend to come in below market value. A 2023 MarketWatch analysis found that iBuyer sellers netted 3%–5% less than comparable homes sold on the open market. This option makes sense when speed matters more than price.

### 6. Explore Dual Agency

Dual agency — one agent representing both buyer and seller — can lower total commission since only one agent is involved. But most consumer advocates, including the Consumer Federation of America, warn against it. The agent can't fully advocate for both sides at once. Dual agency is also illegal in eight states: Alaska, Colorado, Florida, Kansas, Maryland, Texas, Vermont, and Wyoming (as of 2025).

## Commission vs. Net Proceeds: Every Cost That Reduces Your Take-Home

Commission is the biggest transaction cost, but not the only one. Here's the full picture.

| Cost Category | Typical Amount |
|---|---|
| Total Agent Commission | 4.5%–5.5% of sale price |
| Title Insurance & Fees | $1,000–$3,000 |
| Escrow Fees | $500–$2,000 |
| Transfer Taxes | Varies by state (0%–2.2%) |
| Prorated Property Taxes | Varies |
| Repair Credits / Concessions | Negotiated |
| Mortgage Payoff Balance | Your remaining balance |

Use this formula to estimate your net proceeds:

**Net Proceeds = Sale Price – Total Commission – Closing Costs – Mortgage Payoff – Concessions**

For example: sell for $500,000 with a 5% commission ($25,000), $9,000 in closing costs, a $300,000 mortgage payoff, and $3,000 in buyer repair credits. Your net proceeds are $163,000.

Transfer taxes deserve a close look because they vary a lot. In Pittsburgh, PA, the combined transfer tax is 4% — among the highest in the country. In most Texas counties, there is no transfer tax at all. Check your county's rate before listing. A surprise five-figure tax bill late in the process is a bad way to find out.

## Commission Calculator for Buyers: What You Might Pay in 2025

The NAR settlement means you, as a buyer, may need to pay your agent's commission directly. Before August 2024, sellers almost always covered this through the MLS. Now you need to know what your agent charges and who's on the hook for it.

Use the calculator above from the buyer's side: enter the purchase price, then input your buyer agent's rate — typically 2%–2.5% — to see the dollar cost. On a $350,000 home with a 2.5% buyer agent fee, that's $8,750.

A few ways to manage this cost:

- **Negotiate your buyer agent's rate** — especially on higher-priced homes where the dollar amount is already large.
- **Ask the seller for a concession** to cover your agent's fee. Many sellers still prefer to offer this because it keeps their listing attractive to more buyers.
- **Finance the fee into your mortgage** — some lenders allow this, but it raises your loan balance and total interest. On a 30-year mortgage at 6.5%, financing an $8,750 agent fee adds roughly $10,700 in interest over the life of the loan.

If a seller is already offering a 2% buyer agent concession and your agent charges 2.5%, you only cover the 0.5% gap — $1,750 on a $350,000 home.

First-time buyers should read the buyer representation agreement carefully. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) says buyers are not required to sign an exclusive agreement with the first agent they meet. The rate, duration, and cancellation terms are all negotiable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the average real estate commission in 2025?
The average total commission in 2025 is roughly 4.5%–5.4% of the sale price, down from 5%–6% before the 2024 NAR settlement (NAR, 2024 Member Profile). Rates vary by market and are more open to negotiation than they were a few years ago.

### How do I calculate real estate commission on a home sale?
Multiply your sale price by the commission percentage. A $350,000 home with a 5% total commission equals $17,500. The calculator above does this instantly and breaks down each agent's share separately.

### Do sellers still have to pay the buyer's agent commission in 2025?
No. As of August 2024, sellers are no longer required to offer buyer agent compensation through the MLS. Many still do, because it attracts more buyers and showings. But it's no longer mandatory — it's a choice.

### Can I negotiate my real estate agent's commission?
Yes. Rates are not set by law or by the National Association of Realtors. In competitive markets or on higher-priced homes, many listing agents will accept 2%–2.5% instead of the traditional 3%.

### What is a typical commission split between listing and buyer agents?
Before the settlement, the total commission was typically split roughly 50/50 between agents. In 2025, each side is negotiated on its own. A common setup is 2.5%–3% to the listing agent and 2%–2.5% offered to the buyer's agent. There is no required split.

### How much does a real estate agent actually take home from the commission?
Most agents split their commission with their broker and keep 60%–80% of their portion. On a $10,000 commission with a 70/30 split, the agent nets $7,000 before taxes, marketing costs, E&O insurance, and MLS dues. After those, take-home is often closer to $5,000–$5,500.

### Is a 6% real estate commission still common in 2025?
Much less so. The NAR settlement and competition from discount brokerages have pushed the average below 6%. Most sellers in 2025 pay between 4.5% and 5.5% total (Clever Real Estate, 2025). Accepting 6% without negotiating means leaving money on the table.
